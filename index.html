Your Program
Why you train
Your exercise physiologist built this program with a specific order and purpose. Understanding it helps you get more out of every session.
This program is built in phases. Phase 1 is built around control before power, and power before speed. That order matters — rushing to speed without the foundation underneath creates poor movement patterns that are hard to unlearn, and raises injury risk significantly. You are building the base that everything else will sit on top of.
Phase 1 — Building goals
Goal 1
Hip stability & control
Your hips are the engine of every athletic movement. Before you can run fast or absorb force well, you need to control your hips under load. Most young athletes skip this step — and pay for it later with knee, ankle and lower back problems.
Goal 2
Produce & absorb force well
Strength is about more than lifting — it is about what your body does when forces are applied to it. Landing safely, decelerating quickly and changing direction without collapsing all come from building this foundation correctly.
Goal 3
Produce force fast — increase your 5–15m speed
Speed is a skill built on top of strength and control. Once your hips are stable and your body can produce and absorb force reliably, you will start expressing that as explosive acceleration over short distances — the most game-changing physical quality in almost every sport.
Ongoing priorities

Recovery & health between sessions
What you do between sessions determines how much you get from them. Sleep, nutrition, hydration and screen habits directly affect how quickly you improve and how resilient your body becomes. Head to the Learn tab for what actually matters and why.
Recovery & Health
Train smart
What you do outside your sessions is just as important as the sessions themselves.
😴
Sleep
Why it matters: Sleep is when your muscles actually grow and repair. During deep sleep your body releases human growth hormone — the signal that rebuilds everything you broke down in training. Without enough sleep you can do all the right exercises and still get weaker, not stronger.

What to aim for: 8–10 hours every night. Not nearly 8. Consistent, real sleep.

The screen connection: Your phone before bed delays sleep onset by 30–60 minutes even when you feel tired. Putting it down 30 minutes early is one of the highest-leverage habits you can build right now.
Even one night of poor sleep measurably reduces reaction time, mood and muscle recovery.
🍑
Nutrition
Why it matters: Food is fuel and building material simultaneously. Protein rebuilds muscle fibres. Carbohydrates power your training. Healthy fats support joints and hormones.

For teen athletes: You need more food than most people your age — your body is growing and training at the same time. This is not the time to eat less. It is the time to eat well.

Focus on: Protein with every meal (eggs, chicken, fish, Greek yoghurt, legumes). Colour from vegetables and fruit. Real carbohydrates like rice, oats and sweet potato.
Eat protein within 30–60 minutes of finishing a session to start muscle repair.
💧
Hydration
Why it matters: Your muscles are roughly 75% water. Even mild dehydration — just 2% of body weight — reduces strength output, coordination and focus.

What to aim for: Around 2–2.5 litres per day as a baseline, more on training days or hot days. Urine should be pale yellow.

The simplest system: A 750ml bottle with you from the moment you wake up. Refill it twice and you are there.
Drink water before you feel thirsty — thirst is already a sign you are behind.
📷
Screen time
Why it matters: Screens affect recovery in two real ways: they delay and reduce sleep quality, and they raise cortisol (a stress hormone) which slows muscle repair and increases anxiety over time.

The specific issue: Social media is engineered to keep you scrolling. Over hours of use that adds up to a nervous system that never fully settles into recovery mode.

It is not about quitting screens: It is about being intentional. Training hard while spending 4–5 hours passively on your phone creates a real ceiling on your results.
Try: 1 hour of movement for every 2 hours of recreational screen time.
